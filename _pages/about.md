“While networks are often invisible, they nevertheless are important sources of our sociability, information, and social capital,” caution Lee Rainie and Barry Wellman in Networked: The New Social Operating System (2012), one of many sociological classics that have directed my research interests toward the issue of “connections” in the networked age. In brief, my research aims to understand how digital networks boost the power of online communities, influence the way that social actors create meaning with each other, and ultimately reshape people's social lives. 

I like the word “connection.” It exemplifies a particular Zeitgeist that dominated the first years of the third millennium, a time when I grew up as the so-called Connected Generation. My personal penchant for journalism and communication became so natural as the area fed three of my passions – a voracious appetite for news and information, a fascination with emerging technology, and more importantly, an idealism to cross the lines of differences, connecting diverse communities and cultures together.
